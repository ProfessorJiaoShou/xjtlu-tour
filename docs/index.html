Project Overview

Smart Tour XJTLU is a lightweight web-based campus guide that integrates 2D/3D immersive navigation with a zone-based resource sharing community. The platform is designed to support both on-site wayfinding and remote campus exploration, providing users with an intuitive and engaging experience.

The project addresses two major challenges identified through user research. First, new students and visitors often struggle with navigation due to the limitations of traditional 2D campus maps, which lack spatial clarity. Second, valuable campus knowledge— such as study materials, daily life tips, and location-specific advice—is scattered and difficult to access.

To solve these issues, we developed an integrated platform that allows users to seamlessly switch between 2D and 3D map views while accessing a community-driven blog linked to specific campus zones. This transforms the campus map from a static navigation tool into an interactive, human-centered digital ecosystem that supports both navigation and knowledge sharing.

1. Motivation & Research

The Why

Our team chose the Campus Tour theme to address a clear and recurring issue identified through user research: new students and visitors often struggle with navigating XJTLU’s campus. Existing solutions, such as static 2D maps, fail to provide sufficient spatial understanding or intuitive guidance in real-world contexts. In addition, useful campus-related knowledge—including study tips, facility guidance, and location-specific advice from senior students—is scattered across different platforms and is difficult to access in a structured way. These two problems together create both navigational and informational barriers for users.

We selected the Heritage track because we interpret campus heritage as not only physical buildings, but also the shared knowledge, experiences, and culture formed by its community. Based on this perspective, we designed Smart Tour XJTLU as an integrated platform that supports both navigation and knowledge sharing. The system provides a switchable 2D and 3D map interface to improve spatial understanding, helping users orient themselves more effectively on campus.

In addition, we introduced a zone-based community blog linked to specific campus areas. This allows users to access and contribute location-related information, ensuring that practical knowledge is preserved and continuously updated by the community. The platform is implemented as a lightweight web application that supports both remote exploration and on-site usage without installation. Overall, the design aims to improve navigation efficiency while also supporting the long-term circulation of campus knowledge and user experience.

Stakeholders

Our stakeholder definition is based on two research questions: students’ willingness to use a campus tour guide system and the types of information they expect from it. The results help identify different user needs within the campus population.

Primary stakeholders are students who are willing to use a campus tour guide system, mainly new or less familiar users. They need clear and efficient navigation support to help them locate buildings, understand campus layout, and access essential services. Their responses show a strong demand for simple and reliable wayfinding assistance.

Secondary stakeholders include students who may not actively use the system but still seek specific campus information. They are more interested in contextual and practical content, such as location details, daily life guidance, and zone-based knowledge rather than pure navigation functions.

This distinction between usage willingness and information needs helps define system priorities: supporting core navigation tasks for active users while also providing useful contextual information to engage a broader student audience.
